# Audio sources

All shipped audio is generated offline and committed under `public/assets/audio`; no provider credential reaches the browser. `scripts/generate_voice.mjs` extracts the canonical spoken text directly from chapter content and the frozen Chapter Three and Four voice catalog, pins the selected ElevenLabs voice identifiers by role, and writes idempotently. `scripts/generate_sfx.mjs` and `scripts/generate_music.mjs` do the same for effects and music. The generators accept chapter filters from 1 through 4 so one chapter can be rebuilt without disturbing another.

Browser text-to-speech is never an audio source or a placeholder. Every line that should speak must have a generated or family-recorded file committed here and covered by the asset and transcription checks. If a recording is unavailable at runtime, the readable caption remains authoritative and the game may play a short nonverbal chime; it must never hand the words to a browser or operating-system voice.

The narrator uses David, a warm British storyteller. Hagrid uses Scottish Paisley (`YIn3yKpQSeXNJMF5CIuj`), a middle-aged male professional voice labeled Scottish, raspy, gruff, and rough while remaining polite. The wandmaker uses Frederick Surrey, the tailor and Violet use Lily, and the keeper uses Alice. These are source-production identifiers only and are never exposed as character claims in the game.

Chapter 2 keeps Violet silent and gives every speaking role a distinct performance profile. The conductor uses George. Harry uses Echo, an English teen performance with only a four-percent offline lift to bring him closer to eleven without making him cartoony. Ron uses Valf, a naturally very young and playful character voice. Hermione uses Emmaline, a bright young British girl performance. The trolley witch uses Alice; the deputy head uses Scottish Paisley; the Sorting Hat uses a lowered, theatrical treatment of Adam Stone; the headmaster uses Frederick Surrey; and objectives, recaps, chapter cards, and chapter narration continue to use David. Any pitch treatment happens only during offline mastering and remains deliberately restrained.

Every newly generated voice file is gently compressed and mastered to approximately -16 LUFS with a -1 dB true-peak ceiling before it is committed. Music is mastered around -22 LUFS and sound effects around -18 LUFS; sparse transient effects may measure a few decibels lower so their peaks remain gentle. A single role can be rebuilt without disturbing the rest of the cast with `npm run assets:voice -- --force --role guide`. Passing `--qa` also transcribes the selected files with Scribe v2, writes their records beneath `audio/qa`, and updates the byte and SHA-256 bindings in the QA manifest.

All six regenerated Hagrid lines were transcribed back with Scribe v2 under `audio/qa/guide`; each retained the complete authored instruction, including Violet’s name and Platform Nine and Three-Quarters. `audio/qa/guide/loudness.json` records the post-master measurements and the previous introduction baseline.

Run `npm run check:voice-qa` to compare the committed transcription records with all currently authored spoken text. The command derives its expected lines from game content and the frozen voice catalog, uses `audio/qa/manifest.json` to associate raw transcription files with voice asset keys and bind each transcript to the exact MP3 by byte size and SHA-256, ignores punctuation, letter case, harmless hesitation spelling, and inaudible British-versus-American spelling differences, and exits unsuccessfully when audio changes after transcription, a speech asset lacks a transcript, or a recording says materially different words. All 117 currently authored lines are covered.

Run `npm run check:audio` to measure every committed MP3 with ffmpeg and enforce the category mix plus a hard true-peak ceiling. Both voice transcription QA and loudness QA run inside the production build, so a changed or unmastered asset cannot deploy unnoticed.

Music prompts explicitly require an original composition and prohibit quotation, imitation, or evocation of any existing film, game, fantasy franchise, composer, score, or melody. Chapter 2’s first pass contains the platform journey, lake reveal, Sorting ceremony, and Gryffindor common-room cues alongside four new interaction effects; the previously established train whistle remains available to the platform scene.
