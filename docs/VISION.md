# Vision

## What this is

Violet asked her dad for a Harry Potter game. This is that game: a point-and-click adventure-RPG where **Violet herself** — not Harry — is a first-year at Hogwarts. She gets the letter, buys the wand, gets sorted, learns spells one class at a time, and at the end of the year she faces Voldemort in the Mirror chamber and beats him.

The fantasy is not "playing Harry Potter." The fantasy is **Violet goes to Hogwarts**. Every design choice bends toward making her feel like the story is happening to her.

## Who it's for

One player. Violet, age 6:

- **Near-zero video game experience.** Every convention we take for granted (health bars, inventories, menus, dual-stick anything) is unlearned. One finger does everything.
- **Reads 1–3 easy words at a time.** Dialogue is voice-first; text is short caption chips that support her reading without gating on it.
- **Knows the first book (~2/3, read aloud) and the first two films.** Content references book 1 / film 1; film 2 contributes the Dueling Club and Expelliarmus. If she wouldn't recognize it, it doesn't go in.
- **Low frustration tolerance.** Frustration is a design bug, not a difficulty setting. She should feel challenged *at moments she chooses* (the Voldemort fight) and never trapped, lost, or punished.
- **Plays on iPad**, mostly with dad, sometimes alone — the game must be fully self-guiding by voice. Sessions run 20–30 minutes, occasionally 1–2 hours; stopping must always be safe, and returning after a week must re-orient her automatically.

## Product pillars

1. **Violet goes to Hogwarts.** She is the protagonist. Canon characters orbit her story.
2. **Complexity and richness over difficulty.** Depth comes from spells-as-verbs, secrets, collectibles, and characters — never from punishing execution or reflex demands.
3. **Voice-first, reading-light.** Every line is spoken aloud (British accents). Captions are 1–3 easy words that build her reading rather than test it.
4. **Never stuck, never punished.** Losing is funny and instantly retryable with progress kept. Hints escalate automatically. There is no way to ruin anything.
5. **Her save is sacred.** A persistent witch who remembers everything across months of play and development. Accidental-wipe-proof.
6. **Secretly a school, never a lesson.** It's set at Hogwarts, so learning hides in plain sight: new spells are *spelled*, potion recipes are read and counted, her own name is on the envelope. The learning layer is subtle, diegetic, and dosage-capped — the moment anything smells like homework, it's a design bug. (Full rules in [GAME_DESIGN.md](GAME_DESIGN.md#the-learning-layer).)

## The emotional target

Wonder, coziness, and bravery. The castle at night should feel *magical*, not scary. The villain should feel beatable-scary — hammy menace she can be brave against, with one genuinely dramatic beat at the climax so the victory lands. When she wins, the game should throw her the biggest celebration in the family's game catalog.

## IP stance

Private, non-commercial, never published or sold — a fan work for one child. Names, spell names, places, and story beats from the books are used freely. Hard lines, held everywhere:

- No reproduction of passages from the books (all dialogue and the Hogwarts letter are original text).
- No assets from the films or games — no music, fonts, stills, or sound recordings.
- Generated art is original storybook illustration; no actor likenesses.
- The John Williams theme is not quoted; the score is original "wonder and candlelight" orchestration.

## Definition of done

Chapter 1 (The Letter & Diagon Alley) fully playable with final art, voice, and music, verified on the actual iPad — then chapters ship one at a time through Chapter 8 (Voldemort & the House Cup). The real acceptance test at every milestone is a Violet playtest: does she understand it without being told, does she smile, does she ask to keep playing?
